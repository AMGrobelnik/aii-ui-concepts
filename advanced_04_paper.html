1. Introduction

Cross-lingual transfer learning has emerged as a promising paradigm for extending the reach of NLP systems to the thousands of languages for which labeled training data is scarce or nonexistent [1] [2]. Multilingual pre-trained models such as mBERT and XLM-RoBERTa have demonstrated remarkable zero-shot transfer capabilities, yet their performance degrades significantly for low-resource languages that are underrepresented in the pre-training corpus [3]. This performance gap is exacerbated for typologically distant language pairs, where structural and lexical divergences impede effective representation sharing.

In this work, we propose a two-stage approach that addresses these limitations. First, we construct synthetic code-switched corpora that expose the model to fine-grained lexical correspondences between high-resource and low-resource languages. Second, we introduce a contrastive pre-training objective that leverages these code-switched sequences alongside parallel data to learn tightly aligned cross-lingual representations. Unlike prior code-switching augmentation methods that operate at the sentence level [4], our approach performs linguistically informed token-level substitution, preserving syntactic structure while maximizing lexical exposure.

2. Related Work

Recent advances in multilingual pre-training have established strong baselines for cross-lingual transfer [1] [5]. Conneau et al. introduced XLM-RoBERTa, demonstrating that scaling multilingual pre-training data significantly improves downstream performance. Code-switching as a data augmentation strategy has been explored in several works, though primarily for bilingual settings. Yang et al. [4] proposed random token replacement for augmenting NER training data, while Qin et al. studied phrase-level code-switching for machine translation. Contrastive learning for cross-lingual alignment has been explored by Chi et al. [6], who proposed InfoXLM with a cross-lingual contrastive task. Our work differs in combining synthetic code-switching with contrastive pre-training in a unified framework, and in targeting low-resource languages specifically.

5. Conclusion

We have presented XL-CONTRAST, a contrastive pre-training framework that leverages synthetic code-switching to improve cross-lingual transfer for low-resource languages. Our experiments demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods across multiple tasks and languages, with the largest gains observed for typologically distant and extremely low-resource languages. The success of our approach suggests that linguistically informed data augmentation, when combined with representation alignment objectives, can substantially narrow the performance gap between high- and low-resource languages. Future work will explore extending our framework to generative tasks and investigating its applicability to language families not covered in current benchmarks.
